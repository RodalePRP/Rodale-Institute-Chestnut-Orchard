Featured Chestnuts in the Orchard

Amy

‘Amy’ (C. mollissima) is a G. Miller selection (72-400) from a 1972 planting of C. mollissima seedlings in Carrollton, Ohio, from the same planting that produced ‘Miller 72-76’, ‘Gideon’, and ‘Peach’. Its mother was acquired from Ackerman Nursery, Bridgman, Michigan, in 1957. ‘Amy’ displayed greater cold hardiness than most other C. mollissima trialed in Carrollton and breaks bud relatively early in spring. It consistently bears high yields. DNA markers indicate that it is approximately one-eighth Japanese, likely contributed by its unknown father. Its nuts are small to medium, peel well, and have an excellent flavor. ‘Amy’ matures earlier than ‘Eaton’ and ‘Sleeping Giant’, though nut size tends to decline with mature bearing.

AU Cropper

‘AU Cropper’ (C. mollissima) was released by Auburn University in 1980. The cultivar produces heavy crops of decent-quality, medium-size chestnuts. Average nut size can decrease moderately in larger crop years. In Missouri, consistent mature bearing began 8 years from grafting and increased incrementally from 2004 to 2010.

Gideon

‘Gideon’ (C. mollissima) is a G. Miller selection (72-283) from the same seedlot that produced ‘Amy’ and ‘Peach’. It has been a consistent bearer compared with most other C. mollissima trialed in Carrollton, Ohio, and its offspring have also been consistently superior. The cultivar ripens during mid-season, slightly later than ‘Peach’. Nuts are typically 12–18 g, with a uniform and appealing shape.

Hong Kong

‘Hong Kong’ (C. mollissima) is considered the best seedling selection from a seedlot from Hong Kong. The tree is pollen-fertile and drops its nuts during the early to mid-season. The nuts peel well and have very good flavor with a pleasant sweetness. Grafted trees at the University of Missouri grew vigorously and had fair productivity without blight. Nuts from ramets at the UMCA are consistently medium to large in size.

AU Homestead

‘AU Homestead’ (C. mollissima) is a selection from a USDA cooperative planting established at Auburn University in 1954 and released in 1980. It is late-ripening, has a long ripening period, and is considered better suited for household use than commercial plantings. The nut is noted for exceptionally fine flavor and a glutinous quality preferred in China, contributing a rich and complex mouthfeel. ‘AU Homestead’ often tastes much better roasted than raw, and nut size fluctuates with crop size and tree age.

Mossbarger

‘Mossbarger’ (C. mollissima) is a 1983 selection from Kentucky. The cultivar is presumed to be pure C. mollissima, though other species may be present in its background. Although the tree looks predominantly like C. mollissima, ‘Mossbarger’ and its offspring have unusually thick stems and branches. Nuts have very good flavor, whether raw or cooked. The cultivar consistently yielded large crops in UMCA and UTC trials, and ramets are quite vigorous and precocious compared with most C. mollissima selections.

Peach

‘Peach’ (C. mollissima) is a G. Miller selection from a 1972 planting of seedlings in Carrollton, Ohio, from the same planting that produced ‘Miller 72-76’, ‘Amy’, and ‘Gideon’. Its primary attribute is consistent production of medium to large nuts with distinct “peach fuzz” on the shell. The tree tends to fall flower, which can reduce the next year’s yield. Flavor is considered typical for the species.

Payne

‘Payne’ (C. mollissima), also known as Byron 3-3, was selected from the old orchard at the Southeastern Fruit and Tree Nut Research Laboratory in Byron, Georgia. It was chosen for its large nuts, although grafted trees have not always reproduced the nut size of the original ortet. In Missouri, nut size decreases from about 19 g to 10–13 g as trees mature. It was one of the largest-fruited and highest-yielding C. mollissima cultivars in the UTC trial. Nuts drop from mid- to late season and have good flavor.

Qing

‘Qing’ (C. mollissima) was discovered as a yard tree in Hickory, Kentucky, where it was planted sometime between the late 1950s and early 1960s. Its origin is unknown, but it may have originated from trees distributed by the Tennessee Valley Authority. Compared with adjacent C. mollissima trees, it is a much smaller tree with a desirable loose canopy architecture. It is a heavy producer, and its shiny dark-brown nuts fall during the early to mid-season. ‘Qing’ nuts have good flavor, are exceptionally sweet, store very well, and show minimal blossom end rot. Grafts often experience delayed failure or poor growth, but the cultivar has performed highly consistently in UMCA trials.

Sleeping Giant

‘Sleeping Giant’ (C. mollissima × C. crenata × C. dentata) is a seedling derived from a cross made at the Connecticut Agricultural Experiment Station in 1937. It ripens during early to mid-season. Nuts with good flavor are typically 11 to 12 g in northern environments and 13 to 16 g in New Franklin, Missouri. Yields and nut size are relatively consistent, and some growers indicate that the cultivar transfers well between environments. Blossom end rot susceptibility has been observed in Missouri.

Yixin Large Nut

‘Yixian Large Nut’ (C. mollissima) is a selection made in 1993 by M. Nave. The seedling originated from a seedlot of large nuts from Yixian, Anhui Province, China, and is possibly a sibling of ‘Shing’. True to its name, the cultivar produces a large nut, reaching about 20 g in Missouri, with good flavor, although nut size varies by environment. The pellicle removes well, and the nuts drop over a long period from early to late season. More evaluations in the East and Midwest are still needed.
